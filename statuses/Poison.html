Poison (status condition)

Hawlucha poisoned in the anime

The poison condition (PSN) (Japanese: ÊØí poison) is a non-volatile status condition found in the Pok√©mon games, it causes a Pok√©mon to lose HP at the end of every turn, as well as outside of battle prior to Generation V. It can be caused by several moves most of which are Poison-type, and some Abilities. Poison and Steel-type Pok√©mon are immune to being poisoned, making it the only non-volatile status condition to be ineffective against more than one type; however, Poison-type and Steel-type Pok√©mon can be poisoned by Pok√©mon with Corrosion, and in Generation II, Steel-type Pok√©mon can be poisoned by Twineedle.

Leavanny badly poisoned in the anime

Along with the poison status, a Pok√©mon can also be badly poisoned (Japanese: ÁåõÊØí deadly poison); the effects are similar to poison but instead of having a set amount of damage dealt each turn, badly poisoned Pok√©mon will receive increasing amounts of damage each turn. The label for a Pok√©mon that is badly poisoned has light purple text from Generation V on, rather than the white text of standard poisoning.

Effect

The effects of poison vary between generations, but a poisoned Pok√©mon will take damage every turn. Prior to Generation V, outside of battle all poisoned Pok√©mon lose one HP every four steps the player takes. If the player's last conscious Pok√©mon faints this way, they will black out.

Generation I

A poisoned Pok√©mon will take damage equal to 1/16 of its maximum HP every turn, after it attacks, or at the end of the turn if it did not attack. If a poisoned Pok√©mon causes an opponent to faint, the poisoned Pok√©mon will not take damage that turn. Outside of battle, all poisoned Pok√©mon lose one HP for every four steps taken.

A badly poisoned Pok√©mon takes damage equal to 1/16 of its maximum HP on the first turn, after which damage increases by 1/16 each time it takes poison damage. When a badly poisoned Pok√©mon is affected by Haze, switches out, or when the battle ends, its poison status becomes regular poison.

If a Pok√©mon badly poisoned by Toxic is also under the effect of Leech Seed, both types of recurrent damage will draw upon the same N value to calculate how many multiples of 1/16 of the Pok√©mon's HP is taken as damage, and both will increase that value. If a badly poisoned Pok√©mon successfully uses Rest, it will be cured of poison, but N is not reset; if it then suffers burn, Leech Seed or poison damage, that damage will draw upon the N value, and the N value will still increase by 1 each time (however, if the Pok√©mon is poisoned with Toxic, the N value will be reset to 1).

Generation II

A poisoned Pok√©mon will take damage equal to 1/8 of its maximum HP every turn.

Bad poisoning remains at a starting amount of 1/16, and no longer interacts with other types of recurrent damage. Haze no longer affects poisoning. While Steel-type Pok√©mon cannot be poisoned by Poison-type moves, they can be poisoned by Twineedle.

Generation III

Poison damage is now taken at the end of each turn, regardless of whether a Pok√©mon is knocked out.

If a badly poisoned Pok√©mon is switched out, it will remain badly poisoned, but the counter is reset. Bad poison still reverts to regular poison after completing a battle.

Steel-type Pok√©mon can no longer be poisoned by any moves.

Generation IV

Outside of battle, if a poisoned Pok√©mon is brought down to one HP due to poison damage, it will be cured of poison instead of fainting.

Generation V on

A poisoned Pok√©mon no longer receives damage outside of battle.

A poisoned Pok√©mon now glows purple in battle in Generation V, or continuously releases bubbles of poison from its body in Generation VI. Poisoned Pok√©mon take double damage from Hex and Venoshock.

In Generation V, bad poison displays with dark purple characters in the icon instead of white; in Generation VI, both icon and text change color.

A Pok√©mon has a 30% chance of being poisoned after making contact with a Pok√©mon with the Poison Point Ability, and a 9% chance after making contact with a Pok√©mon with Effect Spore. Poison Touch has a 30% chance (20% in the Japanese versions of Pok√©mon Black and White) of poisoning the target when the user uses a contact move. A Pok√©mon can also be poisoned if it directly poisons a Pok√©mon with the Synchronize Ability.

Other causes

The item Toxic Orb badly poisons the holder at the end of the turn. From Generation V onward, a Pok√©mon can also be badly poisoned if it badly poisons a Pok√©mon with Synchronize (prior to Generation V, Synchronize only inflicts regular poison).

Curing

Poison (including bad poison) can be cured with the use of an Antidote, Drash Berry (Generation III only) and Pecha Berry (PSNCureBerry in Generation II). In addition, like all other major status conditions, it can be cured by the items Full Heal, Rage Candy Bar, Lava Cookie, Old Gateau, Casteliacone, Lumiose Galette, Shalour Sable, Big Malasada, Full Restore, Heal Powder, Lum Berry (MiracleBerry in Generation II), and Sacred Ash.

The moves Refresh and Rest remove the poison status condition from the user, while Heal Bell (unless the Pok√©mon has Soundproof as their Ability in Generation III and IV) and Aromatherapy remove it from all Pok√©mon in the user's party. In addition, the move Psycho Shift shifts the poison onto its target (thereby healing the user). In Generation I only, using Haze cures the opponent from poison.

Pok√©mon with Natural Cure will be cured upon switching out, those with the Hydration Ability will be cured whilst it is raining. Pok√©mon with Shed Skin have a 30% chance of being cured every turn, and Pok√©mon with Healer have a 30% chance of curing their allies.

Prevention

In general, Steel- and Poison-type Pok√©mon cannot be poisoned. However, they can be poisoned by Pok√©mon with the Corrosion Ability. Additionally, in Generation II, the move Twineedle can poison Steel-type Pok√©mon. If a Pok√©mon is poisoned while not Poison- or Steel-type (such as while affected by a type-changing move or before evolving into a Poison-type or Steel-type Pok√©mon), it will remain poisoned.

Pok√©mon with the Ability Immunity cannot be poisoned. Pok√©mon with the Comatose Ability and Minior in Meteor Form are completely immune to being poisoned. Pok√©mon with the Ability Leaf Guard will be protected from status conditions in harsh sunlight. The Ability Magic Guard will prevent damage due to poison from being taken in battle; however, it does not prevent the damage from being taken outside of battle.

The moves Safeguard and Misty Terrain (for grounded Pok√©mon) will protect the party from status conditions for five turns. A Pok√©mon behind a substitute cannot be poisoned, except due to Synchronize or a held Toxic Orb.

Advantages

While poisoning and badly poisoning, like all major status conditions, have primarily negative effects, it can be advantageous to be poisoned in certain conditions. Pok√©mon with Guts, Marvel Scale, and Quick Feet will have their Attack, Defense, and Speed increased by 50%, respectively, if poisoned or afflicted by any other non-volatile status condition excluding sleep and freeze; however, in Generation IV, sleep will increase the Attack of Pok√©mon with Guts. Poisoning will increase the attack of a Pok√©mon with Toxic Boost by 50%, and the base power of Facade is doubled (from 70 to 140) when inflicted with poison. A Pok√©mon with Poison Heal will regain 1/8th of its maximum HP at the end of each turn instead of taking damage. When capturing Pok√©mon, the poison status also adds a 1.5√ó multiplier to the catch rate of any given Pok√©mon.

In competitive battling in Generation I, as Pok√©mon were not healed before link battles in the handheld games, players would often enter battles with their Pok√©mon already poisoned, as it prevented them from being affected by other more harmful status conditions; also, poison only inflicted 1/16 of the Pok√©mon's total HP as damage each turn rather than 1/8 as it does from Generation II onward. This tactic was not viable in the Pok√©mon Stadium series, as Pok√©mon were restored to full health before battle in these games.

Other in-game effects

If a poisoned Pok√©mon gains the Ability Immunity through the use of Skill Swap, Trace or another method, the poison or bad poison status will be removed.

In Pok√©mon Emerald, when the player is inside the Battle Pyramid, the types of Pok√©mon encountered on each floor follow a set of categories, on the second floor the player will encounter Pok√©mon that poison as their main tactic.

In the Generation IV games, Pok√©mon Platinum, HeartGold and SoulSilver, at the Battle Arcade, one of the effects caused by the roulette is causing the poison status; Pok√©mon that would normally be immune to poison are unaffected. The poison will last for a single battle.

In the spin-off games

Pok√©mon Mystery Dungeon

Like the main games, Pok√©mon Mystery Dungeon also features both normal poisoning as well as bad poisoning. When a Pok√©mon is poisoned, it takes damage every ten turns and is also prevented from regenerating HP. Poison does not disappear over turns. When a Pok√©mon is badly poisoned, it takes damage every two turns and also prevents regenerating HP. Similar to the main games, the poison conditions do not disappear over turns but can be healed with certain moves or items, and by going to the next floor.

Rumble series

Poison and bad poison (Poisoned and Badly Poisoned when inflicted in-game) are negative statuses in the Rumble series. When a Pok√©mon is Poisoned, its HP will gradually drain at a rate determined by the Power of the Pok√©mon that inflicted it for ten seconds. However, if the affected Pok√©mon is controlled by a player, moving around will make the condition wear off faster, with the minimum duration depending roughly on the Pok√©mon's Speed. While Poisoned, purple bubbles emanate from around the affected Pok√©mon's head. The effects under Badly Poisoned are similar, but the rate at which HP is depleted gradually increases while the status lasts and purple smoke emanates from the Pok√©mon's head instead of bubbles. Though most negative statuses will replace one another if one is inflicted while another is present, Poisoned cannot replace Badly Poisoned (though Badly Poisoned will replace Poisoned).

No types are immune to poison or bad poison, but Pok√©mon with the Poison Boost or Steady Special Traits cannot be poisoned or badly poisoned, and those with the Reflector Trait will cause the user of the poison-inflicting move to become poisoned or badly poisoned instead if hit by one.

Pok√©mon Conquest

Like the main series, a Pok√©mon inflicted with poison is protected from other status conditions and does not wear off over time. Normal poison can be inflicted by attacks, abilities, or by a Pok√©mon ending their turn in a poison bog. Bad poison can only be inflicted by the effect of Poison Fang. As in the main series, Poison- and Steel-types are immune to poison. Poison can be cured through certain Warrior Skills, items, or by ending a Pok√©mon's turn in a hot spring or a water bucket.

Pok√©mon afflicted with normal poison lose 1/8th their max HP, rounded down, at the end of their side's turn, even if the poisoned Pok√©mon itself took no action. Pok√©mon afflicted with bad poisoning lose 1/16th of their max HP initially, with damage increasing by 1/16 at the end of their side's turn. Enemy Warriors defeated through poison damage are not treated as being defeated by the player, and thus cannot be recruited after the battle.

Pok√©mon Shuffle

In Pok√©mon Shuffle, a poisoned Pok√©mon takes 50% more damage from Poison-type Pok√©mon.

Poison can be inflicted by Pok√©mon with the Poison Skill.

Poison, Ground, Rock, Ghost, and Steel-type Pok√©mon are immune to poison.

In the TCG

A poison marker from the TCG.

In the Trading Card Game, Poisoned is one of the five Special Conditions along with Asleep, Burned, Confused, and Paralyzed. When a Pok√©mon is Poisoned, a poison counter is placed on it and one damage counter is put on the Pok√©mon in between each turn. The condition can be removed by returning the affected Pok√©mon to the Bench or by evolving it. Unlike the Pok√©mon games, a Pok√©mon can be afflicted with more than one Special Condition at once; however, some Special Conditions will erase ones already present.
